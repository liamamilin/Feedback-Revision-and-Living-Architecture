10  Chapter 8 — Boundary Revision

“The definition of a boundary is not a constraint; it is a precondition for coherence.” — Geoffrey C. Bowker and Susan Leigh Star, Sorting Things Out (1999)

“To define a thing is to limit it.” — Oscar Wilde

10.1 The Work of Boundaries

Every structure, whether conceptual, organizational, or technical, is defined by its boundaries. A scientific discipline is bounded by its subject matter and methods. An organization is bounded by its charter, geography, and reporting lines. A software system is bounded by its API, its deployment boundaries, and the responsibilities assigned to each module. Boundaries do the essential work of inclusion and exclusion: they determine what belongs and what does not, what is inside and what is outside, what is the structure’s responsibility and what lies beyond its scope. But boundaries that are never revised become prisons. The academic discipline that refuses to engage with adjacent fields becomes sterile. The organization that rigidly maintains its departmental boundaries in the face of changing markets becomes inefficient. The software system whose module boundaries were drawn for a set of requirements that no longer hold becomes a maintenance nightmare. Boundary revision is therefore a central practice in maintaining living structures. This chapter explores the nature of boundaries, why they need periodic revision, the strategies available for adjusting them, the challenge of maintaining coherence through boundary changes, and the ethical dimensions of boundary decisions.

Boundaries serve several critical functions in any structured system. They give a structure its identity, making clear what belongs and what does not. They determine how attention and resources are distributed. They enable the development of depth through specialization. And they define interfaces between systems, making coordination manageable. But boundaries also have a darker side. They can create silos that impede necessary communication. They can become turf battles, defended not because the boundary makes structural sense but because it serves political interests. They can become invisible — taken for granted to the point where their constructed nature is forgotten and they are treated as natural or inevitable. Boundary revision is necessary precisely because boundaries, once established, tend to harden and become invisible, even as the conditions that justified them shift.

10.2 Why Boundaries Need Periodic Revision and Strategies for Adjustment

Boundaries are responses to conditions that change. The case for periodic boundary revision rests on several observations. Ashby’s law of requisite variety states that a control system must be at least as complex as the system it controls. Applied to boundaries, this suggests that the complexity of a structure’s boundaries must match the complexity of its environment. As environments become more complex, boundaries must be revised to maintain the match. Every boundary is drawn based on assumptions about what belongs together, what should be separated, and how the system will interact with its environment. These assumptions have a half-life. The assumption that frontend and backend development should be separate teams made sense when applications were simpler; as applications become more integrated, the boundary may need to shift. Structures tend to accumulate boundaries over time. New departments are created, new categories are added, new modules are introduced, but old boundaries are rarely removed. The result is a structure that becomes increasingly fragmented, with coordination costs rising as boundaries multiply. Periodic boundary revision provides an opportunity to consolidate, remove obsolete boundaries, and rationalize the overall structure. Finally, some phenomena naturally span existing boundaries, and when boundary-spanning phenomena become important, existing boundaries must be revised.

Boundary revision is not a single operation but a family of strategies. Expansion involves widening a boundary to include phenomena that previously lay outside it. The risk of expansion is mission creep — the gradual accretion of responsibilities that dilutes focus and coherence. Contraction involves narrowing a boundary by excluding phenomena that were previously inside. Contraction can be painful because it involves letting go of territory, resources, or responsibilities, but it is often necessary for health. Splitting involves dividing a single bounded unit into two or more units. The challenge of splitting is maintaining coordination between the resulting units. Merging is the opposite of splitting: combining two or more bounded units into one. Merging can be even more challenging than splitting because it involves overcoming established identities, cultures, and practices. Redrawing involves changing the basis on which boundaries are drawn without necessarily changing the number of units. An organization that reorganizes from geographic regions to product lines is redrawing its boundaries. Redrawing is among the most ambitious forms of boundary revision because it changes the fundamental logic of the structure. Each strategy is appropriate for different situations, and the choice of strategy depends on the nature of the misalignment between the current boundaries and the structure’s purpose.

10.3 Maintaining Coherence and the Ethics of Boundary Decisions

Boundary revision, if done poorly, can destroy the coherence that boundaries are meant to provide. A structure that is constantly being reorganized, its boundaries in perpetual flux, provides no stability for its inhabitants. The challenge is to revise boundaries while maintaining the integrity of the structure as a whole. The key insight is that not all boundaries need to change at once. A structure can maintain some boundaries as stable anchors while revising others. This provides continuity: people and processes have fixed points around which they can orient themselves. The stable boundaries provide coherence, while the revised boundaries provide adaptation. In practice, this means identifying which boundaries are fundamental to the structure’s identity and which are more peripheral. Fundamental boundaries should change rarely and only for compelling reasons. When boundaries are revised, the transition should be gradual rather than abrupt. Abrupt boundary changes disrupt ongoing work, create confusion about responsibilities, and generate resistance. Gradual transitions — pilot programs, phased rollouts, transition periods — allow people and processes to adapt. When boundaries are revised, the interfaces across those boundaries must be maintained or redesigned. An interface is the point of contact between the inside and the outside of a bounded unit. If a boundary changes but the interface is not updated, the result is confusion. Boundary objects — artifacts that inhabit multiple boundaries while maintaining different meanings in each, such as a project plan or a shared database — can provide continuity during boundary revision, remaining stable even as the boundaries around them change.

Boundary decisions are never purely technical. They involve judgments about what matters, who belongs, and how resources should be allocated. Every boundary excludes as well as includes. A scientific discipline that excludes certain methodologies effectively silences researchers who use those methods. A classification system that excludes certain categories renders certain experiences invisible. Those who draw boundaries have a responsibility to consider the consequences of exclusion. Boundaries are not neutral; they serve interests. Existing boundaries benefit those who are well-positioned within them. Department heads benefit from the size and scope of their departments. Disciplinary gatekeepers benefit from the boundaries that define their authority. Boundary revision inevitably redistributes power. Those who benefited from the old boundaries will resist revision; those who would benefit from new boundaries will advocate for change. Ethical boundary revision requires awareness of this power dimension and mechanisms for ensuring that boundary decisions serve the structure’s purpose rather than the interests of particular groups. Given the ethical stakes of boundary decisions, they should be made transparently and with participation from those affected. A boundary revision imposed from above without consultation is likely to be resented, resisted, or subverted. A participatory process — where stakeholders have input into boundary decisions — produces better outcomes and greater legitimacy. The willingness to revise boundaries — to question what is inside and what is outside, to redraw lines that have become barriers, to let go of territory that no longer serves the structure’s purpose — is a mark of intellectual and organizational maturity.

10.4 Summary: Knowledge That Endures

Boundary revision is the practice of keeping a structure’s scope aligned with its purpose. Boundaries are not eternal; they are provisional agreements about what belongs together and what should be separated. The strategies of expansion, contraction, splitting, merging, and redrawing provide a toolkit for boundary revision. The challenge of maintaining coherence through revision requires balancing stability and change, managing transitions gradually, maintaining interface integrity, and using boundary objects as bridges. And the ethics of boundary decisions remind us that boundaries are never just technical — they involve judgments about inclusion and exclusion, they distribute power, and they affect real people. The following table captures the essential knowledge that every reader must carry forward from this chapter.

Core Insight What It Means Why It Matters
Boundaries define identity but can become prisons They enable specialization and coordination but also create silos and invisibility Boundaries must be periodically examined, not taken for granted
Four forces drive the need for boundary revision Environmental complexity, decaying assumptions, boundary proliferation, and boundary-spanning phenomena These forces are always operating; ignoring them leads to misalignment
Five strategies comprise the boundary revision toolkit Expansion, contraction, splitting, merging, and redrawing Each strategy suits a different situation; the skill is choosing the right one
Coherence during revision requires deliberate management Stability in core boundaries, gradual transitions, interface integrity, and boundary objects Without these practices, revision destroys the coherence it aims to restore
Boundary decisions are ethically consequential They involve inclusion and exclusion, redistribute power, and affect real people Ethical revision requires transparency, participation, and awareness of power dynamics

The essential lesson that must be committed to memory is this: every boundary is a provisional agreement, subject to revision as conditions change. The willingness to question existing boundaries, to redraw lines that have become barriers, and to let go of territory that no longer serves the structure’s purpose is not a sign of indecisiveness but of structural maturity. Boundaries serve the structure, not the other way around. When a boundary ceases to serve, it must be revised — not defended because it has always been there.

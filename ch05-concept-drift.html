7  Chapter 5 — Concept Drift

“Words have a way of changing their meaning, and the meanings we carry with us are never quite the same as those we received.”
— Jorge Luis Borges, The Library of Babel (1941)

“We do not see things as they are; we see them as we are.”
— Anaïs Nin (adapted)

7.1 Introduction: The Living Word

Concepts are the building blocks of every intellectual structure. A taxonomy is made of them; a software architecture encodes them; an organizational framework organizes them. When concepts remain stable, the structures built upon them appear solid. But concepts are not static. They drift. They change meaning over time, across contexts, and between communities. And when concepts drift, the structures that depend on them drift too — often without anyone noticing.

This chapter examines the phenomenon of concept drift: the gradual, often unnoticed shift in the meaning and use of key terms within a conceptual system. We will explore what concept drift is, why it matters for structural integrity, how to distinguish productive evolution from confusing degradation, and what techniques can help us manage and track meaning changes. Throughout, we will argue that concept drift is not inherently pathological. It becomes dangerous only when it goes unrecognized — when we continue using a term as if it means what it used to mean, while in practice it has come to signify something quite different.

7.2 What Is Concept Drift?

The term “concept drift” originates in machine learning and data mining, where it describes the phenomenon that the statistical properties of a target variable change over time, rendering models that were trained on historical data increasingly inaccurate (Widmer & Kubat, 1996). In the context of data streams, concept drift means that the relationship between input features and the target variable shifts — what was once a reliable pattern no longer holds.

We extend this notion to conceptual structures more broadly. Concept drift occurs when the meaning, scope, or usage of a concept changes over time or across contexts, without a corresponding update to the formal structure that depends on it. The concept is still called by the same name, but it no longer denotes the same thing.

7.2.1 Components of Concept Drift

Concept drift can be decomposed into three related phenomena:

Extension drift occurs when the set of objects or phenomena that a concept refers to changes over time. For example, the concept of “planet” had a stable extension for centuries until the discovery of objects beyond Neptune forced astronomers to decide whether Pluto qualified. The extension of “planet” narrowed dramatically in 2006 when the International Astronomical Union redefined the term, explicitly excluding Pluto.

Intension drift occurs when the defining properties of a concept shift. The concept of “intelligence,” for instance, was once understood primarily as a single, general cognitive ability measurable by IQ tests. Over the twentieth century, it has been extended to encompass multiple intelligences (Gardner, 1983), emotional intelligence (Goleman, 1995), and cultural intelligence (Earley & Ang, 2003). The intension of “intelligence” has broadened considerably, though the term itself remains unchanged.

Valence drift occurs when the evaluative connotation of a concept changes. The term “bureaucracy” once carried neutral descriptive meaning (Weber, 1922); today it is almost exclusively pejorative. Similarly, “hacker” has oscillated between a positive descriptor of skilled programmers and a negative label for malicious actors, depending on context and community (Raymond, 1999).

7.3 Why Concept Drift Matters

Concept drift is more than a semantic curiosity. It has real consequences for structural integrity.

7.3.1 Structural Misalignment

When a concept drifts but the structure built around it remains fixed, misalignment develops. Consider the concept of “user” in software design. Forty years ago, a “user” was typically an individual sitting at a terminal, operating a single application. Today, “users” may be groups collaborating in real time, automated agents interacting via APIs, or even non-human entities consuming data streams. Yet many software architectures still embed the single-user assumption deeply in their data models, authentication systems, and interaction patterns. The concept has drifted, but the architecture has not (Norman, 2013).

7.3.2 Communication Breakdown

Concept drift is a primary source of cross-disciplinary miscommunication. When economists, sociologists, and computer scientists all use the term “network,” they are drawing on overlapping but distinct conceptual histories — and the drift between these uses can lead to profound misunderstandings. Economists think of networks as structures of exchange; sociologists think of them as structures of relationship; computer scientists think of them as structures of data transmission. When these communities collaborate, they must negotiate the drift between their respective concepts, or risk talking past one another.

7.3.3 Hidden Inconsistency

Perhaps most dangerously, concept drift can create hidden inconsistencies within a single system. An organization’s mission statement may use “innovation” in one sense, while its performance evaluation system uses it in another. A legal framework may define “reasonable” in ways that shift across jurisdictions and over time, creating a patchwork of interpretation that undermines the principle of equal treatment (Hart, 1961). These inconsistencies are difficult to detect precisely because the same word is being used — it sounds as though everyone is talking about the same thing.

7.4 Productive Evolution versus Confusing Drift

Not all concept drift is harmful. Indeed, some drift is essential for conceptual vitality. The challenge is distinguishing between productive evolution and confusing drift.

7.4.1 Productive Evolution

Productive concept evolution occurs when a concept’s meaning shifts in ways that enhance its usefulness, clarity, or relevance. This often happens through a process of specification or enrichment. The concept of “sustainability” has evolved from a vague aspiration into a multidimensional framework encompassing environmental, social, and economic dimensions (Elkington, 1997). The term has become richer and more useful as a result.

Productive evolution typically has three features:

  1. It is explicit. The community acknowledges that the concept is being revised, and debates the revision openly.
  2. It is grounded. The revision responds to genuine gaps or problems in the previous conceptualization.
  3. It improves alignment. The evolved concept better captures the phenomena it is meant to describe.

7.4.2 Confusing Drift

Confusing drift, by contrast, is subtle, unacknowledged, and corrosive. It has its own distinctive features:

  1. It is implicit. The meaning shifts without discussion. People use the term differently without realizing they are doing so.
  2. It is ungrounded. The shift is driven not by empirical necessity but by convenience, fashion, or rhetorical advantage. “Disruption” became a fashionable term in the 2010s, applied to everything from ride-sharing to education, losing any precise meaning (Christensen & Raynor, 2003).
  3. It creates confusion. Different people use the term in different ways, leading to systematic misunderstandings that go undiagnosed.

7.4.3 The Boundary Problem

The boundary between productive evolution and confusing drift is not always clear. Consider “artificial intelligence.” The term has undergone multiple waves of meaning change since its coinage in 1956. At various times, “AI” has referred to symbolic reasoning systems, neural networks, expert systems, machine learning, and large language models. Some of these shifts have been productive — the field has genuinely advanced. Others have been driven by hype cycles and funding incentives, leading to what AI researchers call “concept creep” (Haslam, 2016). The term “AI” today is so broad as to be nearly meaningless in casual discourse, yet it retains precise meanings in technical subcommunities. Is this evolution or drift? The answer depends on where one stands.

7.5 Detecting Concept Drift

How can we tell when a concept has drifted? Detection requires both empirical observation and conceptual analysis.

7.5.1 Lexical Tracking

The simplest detection method is lexical tracking: systematically observing how a term is used across contexts and over time. This can be done informally — by noting when colleagues use a key term in surprising ways — or formally, through corpus analysis and natural language processing tools. In organizational settings, tracking how mission-critical terms like “quality,” “efficiency,” or “customer focus” are used in documents, meetings, and communications can reveal hidden drift.

7.5.2 Boundary Mapping

A more analytical approach is boundary mapping: explicitly defining the current boundaries of a concept — its intension and extension — and comparing them with earlier definitions. This requires historical awareness: what did this concept mean when it was first introduced into the system? How has its usage changed? Boundary mapping can be done through interviews with long-standing community members, archival research, or close reading of foundational texts.

7.5.3 Consistency Testing

Consistency testing involves checking whether a concept is used consistently within a single document, conversation, or system. If the same term appears to have different meanings in different contexts within the same framework, drift is likely occurring. For example, a corporate strategy document that uses “innovation” to mean both “incremental product improvement” and “radical business model transformation” in adjacent paragraphs is displaying concept drift that will cause confusion when the strategy is implemented.

7.5.4 The Stranger Test

A useful heuristic is what we might call the stranger test: give a description of your field or system to an intelligent outsider (or better, a newcomer) and ask them to define its key terms. If their understanding deviates substantially from what you intend, the concepts in use may be more fluid than you realize. Newcomers often detect concept drift more readily than insiders because they have not yet internalized the community’s implicit adjustments.

7.6 Techniques for Managing Concept Drift

Once detected, concept drift can be managed, though not eliminated. The following techniques can help keep concepts alive and useful without allowing them to degrade into confusion.

7.6.1 Explicit Definition and Versioning

The most straightforward technique is making definitions explicit and versioning them. When a concept is central to a framework, its definition should be recorded, dated, and updated with rationale. This is standard practice in technical standards (e.g., ISO definitions) and in legal documents (where terms are often explicitly defined for the purposes of a particular contract or statute). The humanities equivalent is the Begriffsgeschichte (conceptual history) tradition, which traces the evolution of key concepts over time (Koselleck, 2002).

7.6.2 Conceptual Audits

Organizations and communities should periodically conduct conceptual audits — systematic reviews of the key terms in their frameworks. A conceptual audit asks:

  • Does this term still refer to what it was originally intended to refer to?
  • Are there multiple, inconsistent uses of this term within the community?
  • Has the term acquired connotations that interfere with its analytical usefulness?
  • Would a new term better capture the current understanding?

7.6.3 Precision Through Context

Sometimes the most effective response to drift is not tighter definition but greater contextual awareness. A concept that has drifted beyond repair in general usage may still be perfectly useful within a specific context. The solution is not to police usage but to make context explicit. In academic writing, this is done through operational definitions — specifying exactly how a term is being used for the purposes of a particular study. In software, it is done through namespaces and scoped identifiers — ensuring that a term’s meaning is always understood relative to its context.

7.6.4 Accepting Ambiguity

Finally, it is worth acknowledging that some level of concept drift is inevitable and even desirable. A living architecture cannot have perfectly fixed concepts any more than a living organism can have perfectly fixed cells. The goal is not to freeze meanings but to maintain enough shared understanding for productive work while allowing room for evolution. The philosopher W. V. O. Quine (1960) argued that meaning is always somewhat indeterminate — that our concepts are underdetermined by the evidence and practices that support them. Accepting this indeterminacy, rather than fighting it, is part of what it means to maintain a living conceptual structure.

7.7 Concept Drift and Structural Adaptation

The relationship between concept drift and structural adaptation is bidirectional. When concepts drift, structures must adapt — but the act of adapting structures also reshapes concepts. This mutual influence is at the heart of the living architecture approach.

Consider the evolution of “database.” In the 1960s, a database was a collection of data organized hierarchically (CODASYL). The concept of “database” then drifted to include relational models (Codd, 1970), then object-oriented databases, NoSQL stores, and eventually a vast ecosystem of specialized data systems. Each structural innovation — each new kind of database — both responded to and further drove concept drift. The concept of “database” today is so expansive that some argue it has become meaningless, while others celebrate its productive diversity.

The lesson is that concept drift and structural evolution are two sides of the same coin. A living architecture embraces this interdependence. It monitors its concepts not to police them but to keep them aligned with the reality they are meant to serve.

7.8 Key Takeaways

  • Concept drift — the gradual change in the meaning, scope, or connotation of key terms — is a natural and inevitable feature of living conceptual systems.
  • Productive evolution enriches concepts and improves their alignment with reality; confusing drift degrades them and undermines communication.
  • The distinction between evolution and drift depends on whether the change is explicit, grounded, and improvements-oriented (evolution) or implicit, ungrounded, and confusion-generating (drift).
  • Detection techniques include lexical tracking, boundary mapping, consistency testing, and the stranger test. Each reveals different aspects of drift.
  • Management techniques include explicit definition and versioning, conceptual audits, context-sensitive precision, and a pragmatic acceptance of inevitable ambiguity.
  • A living architecture treats concept drift not as a problem to be eliminated but as a natural process to be monitored, understood, and channeled toward productive adaptation.

7.9 Exercises and Reflections

  1. Identify a key term in your field or organization that you suspect has drifted. Using the framework of extension drift, intension drift, and valence drift, analyze how its meaning has changed over the past decade. What caused the drift? Has it been productive or confusing?

  2. Conduct a stranger test. Ask someone unfamiliar with your work to read a key document (mission statement, framework description, or technical specification) and then explain in their own words what the central concepts mean. Compare their understanding with your own. What does any divergence tell you about concept drift in your system?

  3. Design a conceptual audit process for a framework you use regularly. What terms would you audit? How would you gather evidence of drift? What would trigger a decision to update a definition or introduce a new term? How would you communicate these changes to the broader community?

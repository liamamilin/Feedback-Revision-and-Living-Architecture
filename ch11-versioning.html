13  Chapter 11 — Versioning

“Every great work of art has two faces: one toward its own time and one toward the future.” — Martha Graham

“Things change. That’s just the way it is. But when you have a system based on consensus and shared understanding, you need a way to track those changes without losing your mind.” — Attributed to Tim Berners-Lee

13.1 The Need for a Map of Structural Time

If iteration is the engine of structural evolution, then versioning is the cartography. It provides the maps, milestones, and markers that allow us to navigate the landscape of a changing structure. Without versioning, iteration produces confusion: users of a framework cannot tell which version they are working with, developers cannot trace when a particular change was introduced, and the community cannot coordinate around a shared reference point. Versioning is, at its core, a response to a fundamental tension in the lifecycle of any living structure. On one hand, the structure must evolve to remain relevant — to incorporate new knowledge, fix flaws, and adapt to changing conditions. On the other hand, the structure must provide stability so that its users can rely on it. A software library that changes every week without warning is unusable; a scientific taxonomy that is revised annually without documentation is untrustworthy. Versioning resolves this tension by naming time. It assigns identifiers to specific states of a structure at specific points in its evolution, creating a shared vocabulary that allows us to refer to “how things were” and “how things are” with precision. This chapter explores why versioning matters for conceptual structures, how different versioning schemes work and when to use them, and the practices that make versioning a tool for clarity rather than bureaucracy.

Versioning is so deeply embedded in software development that its broader applicability is often overlooked. Yet every living conceptual structure that undergoes iterative change faces versioning challenges. Consider a scientific theory that evolves over time. The theory of evolution by natural selection has undergone profound changes since Darwin’s On the Origin of Species in 1859. Scientists speak of “Darwin’s theory,” “the modern synthesis,” and “the extended evolutionary synthesis” as distinct versions of the same underlying theory structure. These version labels enable precise discourse. Without versioning, the theory would exist as an undifferentiated amalgam of propositions from different eras, with no way to refer to its historical states. Versioning serves a coordination function that is especially critical when a structure is used by multiple people or groups. If a team of architects is working from version 2.1 of a reference architecture, they can share assumptions, build consistent artifacts, and communicate without ambiguity. Versioning also serves accountability: when a structure changes, it is important to know what was changed, by whom, and why. In safety-critical domains, the ability to trace which version of a protocol was in effect at a given time can be a matter of life and death.

13.2 Versioning Schemes and the Major-versus-Minor Distinction

Different contexts call for different versioning approaches. Semantic versioning (SemVer) uses a three-part number — MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH — with the rules that major version increments indicate incompatible changes, minor version increments indicate backward-compatible additions, and patch increments indicate backward-compatible bug fixes. SemVer’s genius lies in encoding the nature of the change in the version number itself. A user seeing that a library has gone from 2.3.1 to 2.4.0 knows they can upgrade without breaking their code; if it goes to 3.0.0, they know to prepare for disruption. The SemVer philosophy can be adapted to conceptual frameworks. A major version change would introduce a new foundational concept that reshapes the entire framework or remove a core concept. A minor version change would add a new concept that extends but does not disrupt the existing structure, or clarify ambiguous definitions. A patch would correct factual errors in examples or fix typographical errors. However, SemVer assumes a clear definition of “backward compatibility,” which is more ambiguous in conceptual structures than in software. Defining what constitutes a “breaking change” in a theoretical model requires careful judgment.

Chronological versioning assigns versions based on dates or sequential numbers in order of release. It is simpler than semantic versioning and communicates when a version was released, not what kind of change it contains. This is appropriate for structures where the nature of change is less important than the timeline of evolution. The trade-off is that a user of a chronologically versioned framework knows how recent the version is, but not whether upgrading will be painful. Milestone-based versioning uses labels that mark a significant achievement or stage in development — alpha, beta, release candidate, draft, ratified, edition. These labels convey important information about the status of a version. A “draft” framework is understood to be preliminary and subject to major change; a “ratified” standard carries the weight of consensus. The choice of versioning scheme depends on who uses the structure, how fast it changes, and what information matters most. In practice, many structures use a hybrid approach, combining elements of semantic, chronological, and milestone-based versioning. One of the most practically significant aspects of versioning is determining what qualifies as a major versus a minor change. This determination requires domain-specific judgment. In software, a major version change typically involves removal of an existing API, change in the fundamental data model, or redesign of the user interface. In conceptual frameworks, a major change involves introduction of a new foundational concept, removal or fundamental redefinition of a core concept, or change in the relationship structure between concepts. A persistent challenge is that changes declared as “minor” sometimes have major implications for users. The best safeguard is transparency in the change log, so that even if a change is labeled minor, the accompanying documentation describes its implications honestly.

13.3 Change Logs, Migration Documentation, and the Balance Between Stability and Evolution

A version number is only as useful as the documentation that accompanies it. A change log is a curated, chronologically ordered list of changes for each version of a project. The standard format includes sections for added, changed, deprecated, removed, fixed, and security-related changes. For conceptual structures, a good change log answers the question: “What do I need to know to decide whether to upgrade?” It should be written for humans, not machines, and should link to more detailed documentation where appropriate. For major version changes, a change log is insufficient. Users need migration documentation — a guided walkthrough of the differences between versions and instructions for transitioning. Migration documentation for a conceptual framework should include a summary of changes, a concept mapping table showing how concepts correspond between versions, an impact analysis of what changes when moving from old to new, transition examples showing how to translate work done under the old version, and a deprecation timeline. Beyond its pragmatic function, a change log serves as a record of intellectual development. For a living structure, the change log is its autobiography — the story of how it grew, what problems it encountered, what decisions were made, and why. A user who reads the change log of a framework from version 1 to version 10 gains insight into the framework’s priorities, its blind spots, and the evolving needs of its community.

Versioning is ultimately a tool for managing the fundamental tension between stability and evolution. A structure that never changes provides perfect stability but eventually becomes irrelevant. A structure that changes constantly provides perfect responsiveness but never stabilizes enough to be usable. Versioning does not resolve this tension; it manages it. By providing clear markers of what has changed and when, versioning allows different users to choose their own relationship to structural change. Risk-averse users can stay on stable, older versions and migrate only when necessary. Early adopters can move to new versions quickly, accepting instability in exchange for access to improvements. Maintainers can make breaking changes when needed, knowing that users will be warned through version numbers and migration documentation. The art of good versioning lies in understanding which changes are genuinely necessary, communicating them clearly, and supporting users through transitions. A living structure is not one that never changes but one that changes with discipline, transparency, and respect for its users.

13.4 Summary: Knowledge That Endures

Versioning is the practice of assigning identifiers to specific states of a structure, enabling precise reference across time and among users. It serves coordination, accountability, and communication functions that are essential for any structure that evolves over time. The following table captures the essential knowledge that every reader must carry forward from this chapter.

Core Insight What It Means Why It Matters
Versioning resolves the tension between stability and evolution It names time, creating shared reference points across versions Without versioning, iteration produces confusion rather than learning
Three versioning schemes serve different needs Semantic (communicates nature of change), chronological (communicates timing), milestone (communicates maturity) The choice of scheme depends on the structure’s users, change rate, and information priorities
The major-versus-minor distinction requires domain judgment What constitutes a breaking change depends on the structure and its community Transparency in change logs compensates for the inherent ambiguity of this distinction
A version number without documentation is insufficient Change logs and migration guides are essential companions to version numbers Good documentation answers “what do I need to know to decide whether to upgrade?”
The change log is the structure’s autobiography It records not just what changed but why Understanding revision history enables better judgment about future revisions

The essential lesson that must be committed to memory is this: versioning is not bureaucracy; it is the discipline that makes iteration safe. Without version numbers, change logs, and migration documentation, every revision risks becoming a source of confusion rather than a step forward. The practice of versioning is the practice of treating a structure’s evolution with the seriousness it deserves — documenting what changed, why it changed, and what it means for those who depend on it. A structure that versions well is a structure that respects its users and its own history.

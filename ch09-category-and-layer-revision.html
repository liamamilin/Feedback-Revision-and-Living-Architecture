11  Chapter 9 — Category and Layer Revision

“Things are not ‘naturally’ divided into categories. They are divided according to some principle of classification, and the principle is chosen because it serves some purpose.” — John Dewey, Logic: The Theory of Inquiry (1938)

“The most profound technologies are those that disappear. They weave themselves into the fabric of everyday life until they are indistinguishable from it.” — Mark Weiser (1991)

11.1 The Hidden Architecture of Categories

Categories and layers are the hidden architecture of structured thought. We sort phenomena into categories — customer segments, species, document types, architectural patterns — and we organize those categories into layers — from abstract to concrete, from high-level to low-level, from strategic to operational. These classification systems and layerings are so fundamental to how we think and work that we often fail to notice them. They are, in Weiser’s sense, technologies that have disappeared into the fabric of everyday practice. But like any technology, categories and layers require maintenance. When the world changes, the categories we use to describe it must change too. When the problems we face shift, the layers we use to organize our responses must shift as well. Category and layer revision is the practice of repairing the conceptual infrastructure that structures our work. This chapter explores the anatomy of categories and layers, the signals that indicate they need revision, the methods for repairing broken relational structures, the challenge of backward compatibility, and practical techniques for keeping category systems alive.

A category is a grouping of entities that are considered equivalent for some purpose. Categories are defined by their intension (the set of properties that determine membership) and their extension (the set of entities that belong to the category). Categories serve several functions: cognitive economy (reducing complexity by allowing us to treat many distinct entities as equivalent), communication (providing shared vocabulary for coordinated action), inference (enabling prediction by induction), and evaluation (enabling normative judgment). A layer is a level of abstraction or granularity in a hierarchical structure. Layers organize categories into levels, with higher layers being more abstract, strategic, or comprehensive, and lower layers being more concrete, operational, or specific. Layers serve functions that complement categories: abstraction management (allowing complex systems to be understood at multiple levels of detail), separation of concerns (isolating different kinds of work and responsibility), and progressive disclosure (allowing information to be revealed gradually). Both categories and layers are relational: categories relate entities to each other and to other categories, while layers relate levels to each other through dependency or composition relations. The health of a category-and-layer system depends on the health of these relationships.

11.2 When Categories Need Revision and How to Repair Broken Structures

Category systems, like all structures, decay over time. Recognizing the signs of decay is essential for timely intervention. A category that has become too heterogeneous — containing entities that differ in important ways — needs splitting. Signals include members that differ in ways that matter for how they should be treated, the need for frequent qualification or exception-making, and the category becoming a “grab bag” of entities that do not share important properties. Conversely, categories that have become too fine-grained — where the distinctions between them no longer serve a purpose — need merging. Signals include unclear boundaries that are frequently disputed, entities in different categories being treated the same way, and coordination costs across categories exceeding the benefits of separation. New phenomena, technologies, and practices create the need for new categories, signaled by phenomena that cannot be adequately described within existing categories, the frequent use of “other” or “miscellaneous” categories, and growing frustration with the effort required to fit new phenomena into old categories. And not all categories age gracefully — some become obsolete, signaled by empty or nearly empty categories, categories that are never used in practice, and categories whose maintenance cost exceeds their utility.

Categories do not exist in isolation. They exist in relationship to each other — as hierarchies, taxonomies, or networks. When these relationships break, the entire system suffers. Hierarchical relations break when the “is a” or “is part of” relationship is no longer accurate. A classic example is the classification of tomatoes: botanically a fruit, culinarily a vegetable. A strict hierarchical classification cannot accommodate this dual relationship. The repair involves recognizing that different hierarchies serve different purposes — sometimes the fix is not to repair the hierarchy but to acknowledge its incompleteness. Taxonomic relations — the horizontal relationships between sibling categories — break when the basis of division becomes unclear. Repairing a broken taxonomy often requires clarifying the basis of division. In more complex relational structures such as ontologies and knowledge graphs, relations can break in subtler ways. A relationship that was once valid may no longer hold. A link that was once useful may become misleading. The repair involves ongoing curation: reviewing relationships periodically, removing obsolete links, adding new connections, and validating that the structure still serves its purpose. One powerful approach to repairing relational structures is faceted classification — the practice of describing entities along multiple independent dimensions rather than forcing them into a single hierarchy. This allows for flexible retrieval and resists the brittleness of single-hierarchy systems.

11.3 Backward Compatibility and Practical Methods for Category Revision

Category and layer revision faces a fundamental tension: the desire to improve the system versus the need to maintain compatibility with existing users and data. This is the problem of backward compatibility. When category systems are embedded in tools, databases, and workflows, and when users have invested time and training in the current system, backward compatibility matters. Several strategies can maintain compatibility during revision. Deprecation involves marking a category as obsolete but continuing to support it for a transition period, giving users time to adapt. Mapping provides a way to translate from old categories to new ones, allowing historical data to be reinterpreted in the new system. Versioning the category system, just as software is versioned, provides a framework for managing change. Running two category systems in parallel — the old system for existing users and data, the new system for new use cases — with a gradual migration path, provides the safest approach in some cases. But backward compatibility cannot always be maintained. Sometimes the old categories are so fundamentally broken that patching is impossible. Breaking compatibility should be a deliberate decision, announced well in advance with clear documentation.

Several practical methods support systematic category revision. A category audit is a systematic review of a classification system that examines each category for coverage (does it capture the phenomena it should?), distinctness (is it clearly differentiated from other categories?), utility (does it serve a purpose and is it actually used?), consistency (are the criteria for membership applied consistently?), and balance (is it appropriately sized relative to others?). Card sorting is a technique from information architecture for understanding how people naturally group phenomena, revealing how users think about the domain and informing category revision. Taxonomy governance establishes who can propose category changes, how changes are evaluated, how they are documented, and how they are communicated. A taxonomy governance board — representing stakeholders from different parts of the system — provides oversight and ensures that category revisions serve the whole system rather than particular interests. These methods transform category revision from an ad hoc, reactive process into a systematic, proactive practice. They ensure that categories remain aligned with the phenomena they classify and the purposes they serve.

11.4 Summary: Knowledge That Endures

Categories and layers are the infrastructure of structured thought and action. They organize experience, enable communication, and make complexity manageable. But like all infrastructure, they require maintenance. Categories that were once useful become obsolete. Layers that once clarified become rigid. Relationships that once held become broken. The practice of category and layer revision is the practice of keeping this infrastructure alive. The following table captures the essential knowledge that every reader must carry forward from this chapter.

Core Insight What It Means Why It Matters
Categories and layers are hidden infrastructure They are so fundamental that they become invisible, yet they require maintenance Invisible infrastructure is the most neglected and the most consequential
Categories need splitting, merging, adding, or removing Each operation addresses a specific type of decay Knowing which operation is needed is the core skill of category maintenance
Relational structures break in characteristic ways Hierarchies, taxonomies, and networks each have distinct failure modes Each type of break requires a different repair strategy
Backward compatibility can be managed through multiple strategies Deprecation, mapping, versioning, and dual systems each serve different situations The choice of strategy depends on the costs of change versus the costs of compatibility
Practical methods make revision systematic Category audits, card sorting, and taxonomy governance provide structure Systematic methods prevent revision from being ad hoc or reactive

The essential lesson that must be committed to memory is this: categories and layers are not natural kinds; they are tools created for a purpose. When the purpose changes or the phenomenon shifts, the categories must change too. The practice of category and layer revision is the practice of keeping our conceptual infrastructure aligned with the reality it is meant to organize. It requires periodic audits, a willingness to split and merge, attention to relational structures, and governance processes that balance stability with adaptation. A living structure is one whose categories and layers remain open to revision, capable of reorganizing its own conceptual infrastructure without losing its identity or purpose.

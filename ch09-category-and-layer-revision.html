11  Chapter 9 — Category and Layer Revision

“Things are not ‘naturally’ divided into categories. They are divided according to some principle of classification, and the principle is chosen because it serves some purpose.”
— John Dewey, Logic: The Theory of Inquiry (1938)

“The most profound technologies are those that disappear. They weave themselves into the fabric of everyday life until they are indistinguishable from it.”
— Mark Weiser (1991)

11.1 Introduction: The Hidden Architecture of Categories

Categories and layers are the hidden architecture of structured thought. We sort phenomena into categories — customer segments, species, document types, architectural patterns — and we organize those categories into layers — from abstract to concrete, from high-level to low-level, from strategic to operational. These classification systems and layerings are so fundamental to how we think and work that we often fail to notice them. They are, in Weiser’s sense, technologies that have disappeared into the fabric of everyday practice.

But like any technology, categories and layers require maintenance. When the world changes, the categories we use to describe it must change too. When the problems we face shift, the layers we use to organize our responses must shift as well. Category and layer revision is the practice of repairing the conceptual infrastructure that structures our work.

This chapter explores the anatomy of categories and layers, the signals that indicate they need revision, the methods for repairing broken relational structures, the challenge of backward compatibility, and practical techniques for keeping category systems alive.

11.2 The Anatomy of Categories and Layers

11.2.1 Categories

A category is a grouping of entities that are considered equivalent for some purpose. Categories are defined by their intension (the set of properties that determine membership) and their extension (the set of entities that belong to the category). The category “mammal” has the intension of warm-blooded, hair-bearing, milk-producing vertebrates and the extension of whales, bats, humans, and everything else that meets those criteria.

Categories serve several functions:

  • Cognitive economy. Categories reduce the complexity of experience by allowing us to treat many distinct entities as equivalent. We do not need to learn about every individual customer; we learn about customer segments.

  • Communication. Categories provide shared vocabulary that enables coordinated action. When an engineer says “this is a frontend issue,” the category “frontend” triggers a shared understanding of which team, tools, and processes are involved.

  • Inference. Categories enable prediction by induction. Having classified something as a member of a category, we can infer properties that are typical of that category. If we classify a startup as a “SaaS company,” we can infer its revenue model, growth challenges, and competitive landscape.

  • Evaluation. Categories enable normative judgment. Entities are evaluated differently depending on their category membership. A “bug report” is handled differently from a “feature request” because they belong to different categories.

11.2.2 Layers

A layer is a level of abstraction or granularity in a hierarchical structure. Layers organize categories into levels, with higher layers being more abstract, strategic, or comprehensive, and lower layers being more concrete, operational, or specific.

In software architecture, the classic layered pattern has presentation, business logic, and data access layers. In organizational design, layers correspond to levels of management from CEO to front-line workers. In conceptual frameworks, layers might range from philosophical foundations at the top to practical techniques at the bottom.

Layers serve functions that complement categories:

  • Abstraction management. Layers allow complex systems to be understood at multiple levels of detail. A manager can work at the strategic layer without needing to understand every operational detail. A software developer can work at the business logic layer without managing database connections.

  • Separation of concerns. Layers isolate different kinds of work and responsibility. Changes at one layer should not ripple through all layers. This isolation is what makes layered structures resilient.

  • Progressive disclosure. Layers allow information to be revealed gradually. A high-level view provides orientation; lower layers provide detail when needed. This is essential for managing complexity in both systems and documents.

Both categories and layers are relational: categories relate entities to each other (as members of the same group) and to other categories (through hierarchical relations like parent-child). Layers relate levels to each other (through dependency or composition relations). The health of a category-and-layer system depends on the health of these relationships.

11.3 When Categories Need to Be Split, Merged, Added, or Removed

Category systems, like all structures, decay over time. The signs of decay are recognizable:

11.3.1 Categories That Need Splitting

A category that has become too heterogeneous — containing entities that differ in important ways — needs splitting. In library classification, the category “computers” originally covered everything from mainframes to personal computers. As personal computing evolved, it became clear that the category was too broad. New categories — “microcomputers,” “personal computers,” “mobile devices” — were split off.

Signals that a category needs splitting: - Members of the category differ in ways that matter for how they should be treated - The category requires frequent qualification or exception-making (“X is a mammal, but…”) - The category has become a “grab bag” of entities that do not share important properties - Practitioners regularly create ad hoc subcategories to handle variation

11.3.2 Categories That Need Merging

Conversely, categories that have become too fine-grained — where the distinctions between them no longer serve a purpose — need merging. In organizational structure, two teams that have become tightly coupled in their work may need to be merged into a single team. In software, two modules that are always changed together may need to be merged.

Signals that categories need merging: - The boundary between categories is unclear and frequently disputed - Entities in different categories are consistently treated the same way - Coordination across categories is more costly than the benefits of separation - The categories create artificial distinctions that impede necessary collaboration

11.3.3 Categories That Need Adding

New phenomena, new technologies, and new practices create the need for new categories. The emergence of social media created a need for categories that did not exist before: “influencer,” “viral content,” “engagement metric.” The emergence of cloud computing created a need for categories like “infrastructure as a service,” “platform as a service,” and “software as a service.”

The need for new categories is signaled by: - Phenomena that cannot be adequately described within existing categories - The frequent use of “other” or “miscellaneous” categories - The emergence of practices that feel qualitatively different from existing ones - Growing frustration with the effort required to fit new phenomena into old categories

11.3.4 Categories That Need Removing

Not all categories age gracefully. Some become obsolete as the phenomena they describe disappear. Some were created for purposes that are no longer relevant. Some were mistakes from the beginning — categories that never captured meaningful distinctions.

Categories that need removal are signaled by: - Empty or nearly empty categories - Categories that are never used in practice - Categories whose sole function is to preserve historical continuity - Categories whose maintenance cost exceeds their utility

11.3.5 Case Study: The Dewey Decimal Classification

The Dewey Decimal Classification (DDC) system, first published in 1876, provides a classic case study in category revision. DDC has undergone 23 major revisions, with constant updates between editions. Categories have been split (the 600s, Technology, has been repeatedly subdivided), merged (some area-specific categories were consolidated as global communication improved), added (004-006 for computer science and data processing, a completely new range), and in some cases, quietly removed or reclassified.

The DDC revisions illustrate the tension between stability and currency. Libraries need classification systems to remain stable — re-shelving millions of books is expensive. But they also need them to remain current — misclassifying new materials undermines the system’s utility. The DDC’s revision process, managed by a permanent editorial team with input from librarians worldwide, represents an institutionalized form of category maintenance.

11.4 How to Repair Broken Relational Structures

Categories do not exist in isolation. They exist in relationship to each other — as hierarchies (parent-child), as taxonomies (sibling categories), as ontologies (multi-dimensional relationships), or as networks (associative links). When these relationships break, the entire system suffers.

11.4.1 Broken Hierarchies

Hierarchical relations break when the “is a” or “is part of” relationship is no longer accurate. A classic example is the classification of tomatoes: botanically a fruit, culinarily a vegetable. A strict hierarchical classification cannot accommodate this dual relationship. The repair involves recognizing that different hierarchies serve different purposes — botanical taxonomy and culinary classification are two different relational structures, each valid for its domain.

The general lesson: when a hierarchy breaks, check whether you are trying to impose a single hierarchy on phenomena that require multiple. Sometimes the repair is not to fix the hierarchy but to acknowledge its incompleteness.

11.4.2 Broken Taxonomies

Taxonomic relations — the horizontal relationships between sibling categories — break when the basis of division becomes unclear. If we divide customers into “enterprise,” “small business,” and “consumer,” but some customers fit into multiple categories (is a startup with fifty employees small business or enterprise?), the taxonomy is broken.

Repairing a broken taxonomy often requires clarifying the basis of division. Are we dividing by revenue? By employee count? By buying behavior? By product usage? If the basis of division is clear, borderline cases can be resolved. If the basis of division is unclear, the taxonomy itself is the problem.

11.4.3 Broken Network Relations

In more complex relational structures — ontologies, knowledge graphs, associative networks — relations can break in subtler ways. A relationship that was once valid may no longer hold. A link that was once useful may become misleading. The repair involves ongoing curation: reviewing relationships periodically, removing obsolete links, adding new connections, and validating that the structure still serves its purpose.

11.4.4 The Role of Faceted Classification

One powerful approach to repairing relational structures is faceted classification — the practice of describing entities along multiple independent dimensions rather than forcing them into a single hierarchy. Instead of classifying a book in a single category, a faceted system assigns multiple facets: subject, form, audience, time period, geographic region. This allows for flexible retrieval and resists the brittleness of single-hierarchy systems.

Faceted classification, pioneered by S. R. Ranganathan in library science and now common in information architecture and e-commerce, is itself a response to the limits of traditional category systems. When relational structures break because they cannot accommodate multi-dimensional variety, faceted approaches offer a repair strategy.

11.5 Preserving Backward Compatibility Where Needed

Category and layer revision faces a fundamental tension: the desire to improve the system versus the need to maintain compatibility with existing users and data. This is the problem of backward compatibility.

11.5.1 When Backward Compatibility Matters

Backward compatibility is important when: - The category system is embedded in tools, databases, and workflows - Users have invested time and training in the current system - Historical data is structured using the current categories - The cost of migration is high relative to the benefits of change

11.5.2 Strategies for Maintaining Compatibility

Deprecation. Rather than immediately removing a category, deprecate it — mark it as obsolete but continue to support it for a transition period. This gives users time to adapt and data time to be migrated. Deprecation is standard practice in software APIs and is equally applicable to category systems.

Mapping. When categories change, provide a mapping from old categories to new ones. A mapping table allows historical data to be reinterpreted in the new system and allows old tools to continue functioning while new tools adopt the new categories.

Versioning. Version the category system, just as software is versioned. Semantic versioning — where major versions signal breaking changes, minor versions add categories without breaking backward compatibility, and patch versions fix errors — provides a framework for managing change.

Dual Systems. In some cases, the safest approach is to run two category systems in parallel — the old system for existing users and data, the new system for new use cases — with a gradual migration path from one to the other.

11.5.3 When to Break Compatibility

But backward compatibility cannot always be maintained. Sometimes the old categories are so fundamentally broken that patching is impossible. Sometimes the cost of maintaining two systems exceeds the cost of migration. Sometimes the old categories encode biases, errors, or limitations that must be addressed directly.

Breaking compatibility should be a deliberate decision, not a byproduct of inattention. It should be announced well in advance, with clear documentation of what is changing, why, and how to migrate. And it should be done as rarely as possible.

11.6 Practical Methods for Category System Revision

11.6.1 Category Audit

A category audit is a systematic review of a classification system. The audit examines each category for: - Coverage: Does the category capture the phenomena it should? - Distinctness: Is the category clearly differentiated from other categories? - Utility: Does the category serve a purpose? Is it actually used? - Consistency: Are the criteria for membership applied consistently? - Balance: Is the category appropriately sized relative to others?

Category audits should be conducted periodically — annually for fast-changing domains, less frequently for stable ones. They should involve stakeholders from across the system to capture multiple perspectives.

11.6.2 Card Sorting

Card sorting is a technique from information architecture for understanding how people naturally group phenomena. Participants are given cards representing items to be categorized and asked to sort them into groups. The resulting groupings reveal how users think about the domain and can inform category revision.

Open card sorting (where participants create their own categories) is useful for discovering new category structures. Closed card sorting (where participants sort into predefined categories) is useful for validating existing structures.

11.6.3 Taxonomy Governance

Like model governance discussed in Chapter 7, taxonomy governance establishes who can propose category changes, how changes are evaluated, how they are documented, and how they are communicated. Without governance, category systems evolve haphazardly, accumulating inconsistencies and contradictions.

A taxonomy governance board — representing stakeholders from different parts of the system — provides oversight and ensures that category revisions serve the whole system rather than particular interests.

11.7 Examples from Different Domains

11.7.1 Library Classification

We have already discussed the Dewey Decimal Classification, but library classification more broadly provides rich examples of category revision. The Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) has undergone continuous revision since its inception in 1898. Categories have been added for emerging fields (e.g., “Artificial intelligence” in the 1950s, “Blockchains (Databases)” in the 2010s). Problematic categories have been revised or removed — for example, the heading “Yellow peril” was replaced, and headings related to racial and ethnic groups have been revised to reflect contemporary standards.

Library classification illustrates both the necessity and the difficulty of category revision. The revision of LCSH headings related to race and ethnicity has been particularly contentious, raising questions about whether revision is merely updating vocabulary or whether it involves more fundamental changes to how knowledge is organized.

11.7.2 Software Architecture Layers

Software architecture layers are a prime domain for layer revision. The classic three-tier architecture (presentation, business logic, data) has evolved into more complex patterns. The rise of service-oriented architecture and microservices has challenged the very concept of layers, replacing strict layering with network-like service meshes.

The evolution from layered to hexagonal architecture (ports and adapters) represents a fundamental revision of the layer concept. In hexagonal architecture, the traditional layering is replaced by a structure where the application core is surrounded by adapters that connect it to the outside world. This revision was motivated by the recognition that strict layering created rigidity: dependencies flowed one way, making it difficult to adapt to new interface technologies.

11.7.3 Organizational Hierarchy

Organizational layers — levels of management — are among the most visible and contested category systems in any organization. The trend toward “flatter” organizations represents a layer revision: removing intermediate management layers to accelerate decision-making and reduce overhead.

But layer reduction is not always beneficial. Some organizations that flattened found that they had removed necessary coordination layers and subsequently reintroduced them in different forms. The key insight is that layers serve functions — coordination, escalation, mentoring, decision-making — and that removing a layer requires redistributing those functions to other layers or mechanisms.

The revision of organizational layers is a delicate operation because layers are closely tied to power, status, and compensation. Layer revision is not just structural; it is political and personal. Successful layer revision requires addressing these human dimensions as carefully as the structural ones.

11.8 Conclusion

Categories and layers are the infrastructure of structured thought and action. They organize experience, enable communication, and make complexity manageable. But like all infrastructure, they require maintenance. Categories that were once useful become obsolete. Layers that once clarified become rigid. Relationships that once held become broken.

The practice of category and layer revision is the practice of keeping this infrastructure alive. It requires periodic audits, a willingness to split, merge, add, and remove categories, attention to relational structures, strategies for backward compatibility, and governance processes that balance stability with adaptation.

The examples from library science, software architecture, and organizational design demonstrate that category and layer revision is possible and valuable. But it requires more than technical skill. It requires intellectual humility — the recognition that our categories are provisional, that they reflect choices and values rather than natural kinds. It requires political awareness — the recognition that categories distribute power and status, and that revision is therefore contested. And it requires practical wisdom — the judgment to know when to revise and when to leave well enough alone.

A living structure is one whose categories and layers remain open to revision. It is a structure that can reorganize its own conceptual infrastructure without losing its identity or purpose. This is not easy. But it is essential for any structure that aspires to remain alive.

11.9 Key Takeaways

  • Categories and layers are the hidden architecture of structured systems; they require active maintenance to remain useful.
  • Categories may need splitting (when too heterogeneous), merging (when too fine-grained), adding (for new phenomena), or removing (when obsolete).
  • Relational structures — hierarchies, taxonomies, networks — break over time and require repair through clarifying bases of division, adopting faceted approaches, or ongoing curation.
  • Backward compatibility can be managed through deprecation, mapping, versioning, and dual system strategies, but breaking compatibility is sometimes necessary and should be deliberate.
  • Practical methods for category revision include periodic category audits, card sorting, and taxonomy governance.
  • Examples from library classification, software architecture layers, and organizational hierarchy demonstrate both the necessity and the challenges of category and layer revision.

11.10 Exercises and Reflections

  1. Audit a category system you use regularly. Pick a classification system — a project management board, a file folder structure, a software module organization, a customer segmentation scheme. For each category, assess its coverage, distinctness, utility, consistency, and balance. Which categories would you split, merge, add, or remove?

  2. Map the layers in an organization or system you work with. Identify each layer and its function. Is each layer pulling its weight? Are there too many layers or too few? What would happen if you removed one layer? What functions would need to be redistributed? What resistance would you encounter?

  3. Design a backward-compatible migration path for a category revision you think is needed. What is the current system? What is the desired system? How would you deprecate old categories while introducing new ones? What mappings between old and new would you provide? What versioning scheme would you use? How would you communicate the change to stakeholders?

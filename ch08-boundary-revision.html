10  Chapter 8 — Boundary Revision

“The definition of a boundary is not a constraint; it is a precondition for coherence.”
— Geoffrey C. Bowker and Susan Leigh Star, Sorting Things Out (1999)

“To define a thing is to limit it.”
— Oscar Wilde

10.1 Introduction: The Work of Boundaries

Every structure, whether conceptual, organizational, or technical, is defined by its boundaries. A scientific discipline is bounded by its subject matter and methods. An organization is bounded by its charter, geography, and reporting lines. A software system is bounded by its API, its deployment boundaries, and the responsibilities assigned to each module. Boundaries do the essential work of inclusion and exclusion: they determine what belongs and what does not, what is inside and what is outside, what is the structure’s responsibility and what lies beyond its scope.

But boundaries that are never revised become prisons. The academic discipline that refuses to engage with adjacent fields becomes sterile. The organization that rigidly maintains its departmental boundaries in the face of changing markets becomes inefficient. The software system whose module boundaries were drawn for a set of requirements that no longer hold becomes a maintenance nightmare.

Boundary revision is therefore a central practice in maintaining living structures. This chapter explores the nature of boundaries, why they need periodic revision, the strategies available for adjusting them, the challenge of maintaining coherence through boundary changes, and the ethical dimensions of boundary decisions.

10.2 The Importance of Boundaries in Defining Structure

Boundaries serve several critical functions in any structured system:

Identity and Coherence. Boundaries give a structure its identity. What makes biology distinct from chemistry, or marketing distinct from sales, is the set of phenomena, methods, and questions each considers within its bounds. Without boundaries, structures dissolve into undifferentiated flux. A software module without a clear boundary of responsibility is a module that does everything or nothing — neither is useful.

Resource Allocation. Boundaries determine how attention and resources are distributed. An organization’s departmental boundaries determine who works on what. A scientific field’s boundaries determine which research questions receive funding and which are neglected. A conceptual framework’s boundaries determine which phenomena are analyzed and which are set aside as outside scope.

Specialization and Expertise. Boundaries enable the development of depth. By limiting scope, boundaries allow practitioners to develop specialized knowledge, tools, and methods that would be impossible if every structure attempted to encompass everything. The depth of modern science, the sophistication of modern engineering, the complexity of modern organizations — all depend on well-defined boundaries that allow focused expertise.

Coordination and Communication. Boundaries define interfaces between systems. In software, module boundaries define APIs. In organizations, departmental boundaries define handoff points. In academic disciplines, boundaries define the points of interdisciplinary exchange. Well-designed boundaries make coordination manageable; poorly designed boundaries create friction and misunderstanding.

10.2.1 The Double-Edged Nature of Boundaries

But boundaries also have a darker side. They can create silos that impede necessary communication. They can become turf battles, defended not because the boundary makes structural sense but because it serves political interests. They can become invisible — taken for granted to the point where their constructed nature is forgotten and they are treated as natural or inevitable. Boundary revision is necessary precisely because boundaries, once established, tend to harden and become invisible, even as the conditions that justified them shift.

10.3 Why Boundaries Need Periodic Revision

Boundaries are responses to conditions that change. The case for periodic boundary revision rests on several observations:

10.3.1 The Law of Requisite Variety

W. Ross Ashby’s law of requisite variety states that a control system must be at least as complex as the system it controls. Applied to boundaries, this suggests that the complexity of a structure’s boundaries must match the complexity of its environment. As environments become more complex, boundaries must be revised to maintain the match. A simple departmental structure that sufficed for a stable market may prove inadequate when markets become volatile, globalized, and interconnected.

10.3.2 The Half-Life of Boundary Assumptions

Every boundary is drawn based on assumptions about what belongs together, what should be separated, and how the system will interact with its environment. These assumptions have a half-life. The assumption that a company’s domestic and international operations should be separate divisions made sense when international operations were a small fraction of revenue. As international revenue grows, the boundary may need revision. The assumption that frontend and backend development should be separate teams made sense when applications were simpler. As applications become more integrated, the boundary may need to shift.

10.3.3 The Problem of Boundary Proliferation

Structures tend to accumulate boundaries over time. New departments are created, new categories are added, new modules are introduced, but old boundaries are rarely removed. The result is a structure that becomes increasingly fragmented, with coordination costs rising as boundaries multiply. Periodic boundary revision provides an opportunity to consolidate, remove obsolete boundaries, and rationalize the overall structure.

10.3.4 The Emergence of Boundary Spanning Phenomena

Some phenomena naturally span existing boundaries. Climate change spans geology, atmospheric science, biology, economics, and political science. Customer experience spans marketing, sales, product development, and customer service. Data pipelines span infrastructure, engineering, data science, and business intelligence. When boundary-spanning phenomena become important, existing boundaries must be revised — either by creating new structures that span the boundaries, by redrawing the boundaries themselves, or by creating mechanisms for cross-boundary coordination.

10.4 Strategies for Boundary Adjustment

Boundary revision is not a single operation but a family of strategies, each appropriate for different situations.

10.4.1 Expansion

Expansion involves widening a boundary to include phenomena that previously lay outside it. An academic department that adds a new subfield to its curriculum is expanding its boundaries. A software module that takes on additional responsibilities is expanding its scope. Expansion is appropriate when the phenomena outside the boundary are closely related to those inside and benefit from being handled within the same structure.

The risk of expansion is mission creep — the gradual accretion of responsibilities that dilutes focus and coherence. Expansion must be accompanied by clear criteria for what remains outside even after the boundary widens. An expanded boundary is still a boundary.

10.4.2 Contraction

Contraction involves narrowing a boundary by excluding phenomena that were previously inside. A company that divests a business unit is contracting its organizational boundaries. A software module that delegates a responsibility to another module is contracting its API surface. Contraction is appropriate when a structure has become overextended, when included phenomena are better handled elsewhere, or when focus and specialization are needed.

Contraction can be painful because it involves letting go of territory, resources, or responsibilities. But it is often necessary for health. A structure that tries to be everything ends up being nothing well.

10.4.3 Splitting

Splitting involves dividing a single bounded unit into two or more units. A department that splits into separate teams for different product lines, a software module that is refactored into multiple services, a scientific field that spawns a specialized subdiscipline — all are examples of splitting. Splitting is appropriate when a single boundary contains more variety than can be coherently managed.

The challenge of splitting is maintaining coordination between the resulting units. What was once handled internally within a single boundary must now be handled across a new boundary. Interfaces must be designed, communication channels established, and potential friction anticipated.

10.4.4 Merging

Merging is the opposite of splitting: combining two or more bounded units into one. Merging is appropriate when the boundaries between units have become impediments to necessary coordination, when the work of the units is highly interdependent, or when the cost of maintaining separate structures outweighs the benefits.

Merging can be even more challenging than splitting because it involves overcoming established identities, cultures, and practices. People identify with their units; merging those units can feel like a loss of identity. Successful merging requires attention to the human dimensions of boundary change.

10.4.5 Redrawing

Sometimes boundaries need to shift laterally rather than expand or contract. Redrawing involves changing the basis on which boundaries are drawn without necessarily changing the number of units. An organization that reorganizes from geographic regions to product lines is redrawing its boundaries. A classification system that shifts from alphabetically organized categories to thematically organized ones is redrawing.

Redrawing is appropriate when the current basis of boundary definition no longer aligns with the structure’s purpose. It is among the most ambitious forms of boundary revision because it changes the fundamental logic of the structure.

10.5 Maintaining Coherence While Changing Boundaries

Boundary revision, if done poorly, can destroy the coherence that boundaries are meant to provide. A structure that is constantly being reorganized, its boundaries in perpetual flux, provides no stability for its inhabitants. The challenge is to revise boundaries while maintaining the integrity of the structure as a whole.

10.5.1 Stability and Change in Balance

The key insight is that not all boundaries need to change at once. A structure can maintain some boundaries as stable anchors while revising others. This provides continuity: people and processes have fixed points around which they can orient themselves. The stable boundaries provide coherence, while the revised boundaries provide adaptation.

In practice, this means identifying which boundaries are fundamental to the structure’s identity and which are more peripheral. Fundamental boundaries should change rarely and only for compelling reasons. Peripheral boundaries can be revised more freely as conditions warrant.

10.5.2 The Principle of Gradual Transition

When boundaries are revised, the transition should be gradual rather than abrupt. Abrupt boundary changes disrupt ongoing work, create confusion about responsibilities, and generate resistance. Gradual transitions — pilot programs, phased rollouts, transition periods — allow people and processes to adapt.

Gradual transition also allows for course correction. If a boundary revision is producing unintended consequences, a gradual approach allows the revision to be adjusted or reversed before damage is done.

10.5.3 Interface Integrity

When boundaries are revised, the interfaces across those boundaries must be maintained or redesigned. An interface is the point of contact between the inside and the outside of a bounded unit. If a boundary changes but the interface is not updated, the result is confusion: those outside the unit interact with it based on assumptions that no longer hold.

Maintaining interface integrity during boundary revision requires: - Clear documentation of the new boundaries and interfaces - Communication of changes to all affected parties - Transition support for those who must adapt their interactions - Backward compatibility where possible, or clear migration paths

10.5.4 The Role of Boundary Objects

Boundary objects, as conceptualized by Susan Leigh Star and James Griesemer, are objects that inhabit multiple boundaries while maintaining different meanings in each. A project plan, a shared database, a standardized form — these are boundary objects that facilitate coordination across boundaries. During boundary revision, boundary objects can provide continuity: they remain stable even as the boundaries around them change, providing a bridge between old and new structures.

10.6 Examples from Different Domains

10.6.1 Academic Disciplines

The boundaries of academic disciplines are continuously contested and revised. The emergence of biochemistry in the early twentieth century involved a boundary revision: phenomena that had been claimed by both biology and chemistry were recognized as belonging to a new discipline that spanned the old boundaries. Similarly, cognitive science emerged from psychology, linguistics, philosophy, computer science, and neuroscience — a merger of perspectives that had previously been separated by disciplinary boundaries.

More recently, data science has emerged as a field that spans statistics, computer science, and domain-specific knowledge. Its boundaries remain contested: some see it as a subfield of statistics, others as a new discipline in its own right. The ongoing boundary revision reflects genuine uncertainty about what belongs together and what should remain separate.

These examples illustrate that boundary revision in academia is not merely administrative but deeply consequential. Boundaries determine which research questions are funded, which departments get resources, which students are trained in which methods, and which knowledge is considered legitimate.

10.6.2 Organizational Design

Organizational boundaries are perhaps the most visible and contested form of structural boundaries. Companies frequently reorganize, and each reorganization is a boundary revision: departments are split, merged, created, or eliminated.

A well-documented example is the shift from functional to divisional structures in large corporations during the mid-twentieth century, as documented by Alfred Chandler in Strategy and Structure. Companies that had been organized by function (marketing, manufacturing, finance) reorganized around product divisions, redrawing boundaries to align with market-facing units. This boundary revision was driven by the increasing complexity of multi-product firms, where functional boundaries had become impediments to coordination.

More recently, many technology companies have adopted “squad” models (as popularized by Spotify), where small, autonomous teams are organized around features or services rather than functions. This represents a fundamental redrawing of organizational boundaries — from hierarchical departments to network-like squads, tribes, and chapters.

10.6.3 Software Systems

Software architecture is fundamentally about boundaries. Every architectural pattern — layered architecture, microservices, event-driven architecture, hexagonal architecture — is a set of principles for drawing boundaries between components.

The evolution from monolithic to microservices architecture is a story of boundary revision. In a monolith, boundaries are internal (between modules) but not enforced at runtime. In a microservices architecture, boundaries are elevated to network boundaries between independently deployable services. This revision is driven by the need for independent scaling, team autonomy, and deployment flexibility.

But boundary revision in software is not always toward more granular boundaries. Some systems that over-decomposed into microservices find themselves merging services back together — a boundary contraction in response to the operational complexity of managing too many services. The key insight is that the optimal boundary configuration depends on context and evolves over time.

10.7 The Ethics of Boundary Decisions

Boundary decisions are never purely technical. They involve judgments about what matters, who belongs, and how resources should be allocated. These judgments have ethical dimensions that must be acknowledged.

10.7.1 Inclusion and Exclusion

Every boundary excludes as well as includes. A scientific discipline that excludes certain methodologies effectively silences researchers who use those methods. An organizational structure that excludes certain functions from decision-making effectively disempowers those functions. A classification system that excludes certain categories renders certain experiences invisible.

Those who draw boundaries have a responsibility to consider the consequences of exclusion. Are the excluded phenomena genuinely outside scope, or are they being marginalized? Is the exclusion necessary for coherence, or is it arbitrary? Are there people or perspectives being harmed by the boundary?

10.7.2 Power and Boundary Maintenance

Boundaries are not neutral; they serve interests. Existing boundaries benefit those who are well-positioned within them. Department heads benefit from the size and scope of their departments. Disciplinary gatekeepers benefit from the boundaries that define their authority. Software module owners benefit from the scope of their modules.

Boundary revision inevitably redistributes power. Those who benefitted from the old boundaries will resist revision; those who would benefit from new boundaries will advocate for change. Ethical boundary revision requires awareness of this power dimension and mechanisms for ensuring that boundary decisions serve the structure’s purpose rather than the interests of particular groups.

10.7.3 Transparency and Participation

Given the ethical stakes of boundary decisions, they should be made transparently and with participation from those affected. A boundary revision imposed from above without consultation is likely to be resented, resisted, or subverted. A participatory process — where stakeholders have input into boundary decisions — produces better outcomes and greater legitimacy.

Transparency also means making boundary decisions visible. Too often, boundaries are treated as natural or inevitable, their constructed nature hidden. Making boundaries visible — documenting them, discussing them, questioning them — is a prerequisite for responsible revision.

10.8 Conclusion

Boundary revision is the practice of keeping a structure’s scope aligned with its purpose. Boundaries are not eternal; they are provisional agreements about what belongs together and what should be separated. Like all provisional agreements, they need periodic reexamination and revision.

The strategies of expansion, contraction, splitting, merging, and redrawing provide a toolkit for boundary revision. The challenge of maintaining coherence through revision requires balancing stability and change, managing transitions gradually, maintaining interface integrity, and using boundary objects as bridges.

And the ethics of boundary decisions remind us that boundaries are never just technical. They involve judgments about inclusion and exclusion, they distribute power, and they affect real people. A living structure revises its boundaries not only because conditions change but because it recognizes the provisional and consequential nature of all boundaries.

The willingness to revise boundaries — to question what is inside and what is outside, to redraw lines that have become barriers, to let go of territory that no longer serves the structure’s purpose — is a mark of intellectual and organizational maturity. It is the recognition that boundaries serve the structure, not the other way around.

10.9 Key Takeaways

  • Boundaries define identity, allocate resources, enable specialization, and structure coordination, but they also create silos and become invisible over time.
  • Boundaries need periodic revision because of environmental change, the decay of boundary assumptions, boundary proliferation, and the emergence of boundary-spanning phenomena.
  • Five strategies for boundary adjustment are expansion, contraction, splitting, merging, and redrawing — each appropriate for different situations.
  • Coherence during boundary revision requires balancing stability and change, managing transitions gradually, maintaining interface integrity, and using boundary objects.
  • Boundary decisions are ethically consequential: they involve inclusion and exclusion, distribute power, and affect real people — and should therefore be made transparently and participatively.

10.10 Exercises and Reflections

  1. Map the boundaries of a structure you work within — an organization, a software system, a classification scheme, or a conceptual framework. Identify at least three boundaries that you believe need revision. What strategy (expansion, contraction, splitting, merging, or redrawing) would you apply to each? What would be gained and what would be lost?

  2. Identify a boundary-spanning phenomenon in your domain — something that falls between existing boundaries. How are current structures handling it? What would a boundary revision that better accommodates this phenomenon look like? What resistance would you expect, and from whom?

  3. Consider the ethical dimensions of a boundary decision you have witnessed or been part of. Who benefited from the existing boundaries? Who was harmed or excluded? Was the boundary decision made transparently? How could the process have been more equitable? What would a more ethical boundary revision process look like?

8  Chapter 6 — Structure Drift

“Things do not change; we change.”
— Henry David Thoreau, Walden (1854)

“Every organization necessarily becomes what it does, regardless of what it was created to do.”
— A principle of organizational ecology (adapted)

8.1 Introduction: The Architecture That Forgot What It Was For

Every conceptual structure is created with a purpose. A software architecture is designed to support a particular set of use cases. An organizational department is formed to fulfill a specific function. A theoretical framework is built to explain a defined domain of phenomena. But over time, without any deliberate decision, structures can silently change what they are about. They begin to serve different purposes, address different questions, or encompass different objects, all while retaining their original names and formal descriptions. This is structure drift.

Structure drift is distinct from concept drift (Chapter 5). Concept drift concerns the shifting meaning of individual terms within a framework. Structure drift concerns the shifting identity of the framework itself — its object, its purpose, its scope, its fundamental orientation. A structure that has drifted is not necessarily broken; it may be highly functional in its new role. But it has become something different from what it was, often without anyone noticing or deciding. The danger lies in the gap between what the structure claims to be and what it actually is.

This chapter explores the phenomenon of structure drift: how it happens, why it is dangerous, and how to detect and manage it. We will examine examples from organizational life, software engineering, and scientific communities. We will argue that regular structural health checks are essential for maintaining a living architecture, and we will distinguish between two responses to drift: re-centering the structure on its original purpose versus accommodating the drift as legitimate evolution.

8.2 The Mechanics of Structure Drift

Structure drift is not a single event but a gradual process that unfolds through several mechanisms.

8.2.1 Scope Creep

The most familiar form of structure drift is scope creep. A structure designed for one purpose gradually expands to encompass related — and then not-so-related — functions. In organizations, this is the department that was created to handle customer support but now also manages product documentation, user research, quality assurance, and community management. Each expansion is individually sensible — it makes sense to colocate related functions. But cumulatively, the department’s identity has fundamentally shifted.

In software architecture, scope creep manifests as the framework that was designed for microservices but has grown to include a message queue, a workflow engine, a monitoring system, and a deployment pipeline. Each addition was a reasonable extension of the original vision, but the whole has become something very different from what was initially designed (Buschmann et al., 1996).

8.2.2 Function Creep

Related to scope creep is function creep — a term often used in privacy and surveillance studies (Dahl & Sætnan, 2009). Function creep occurs when a technology or system designed for one purpose is gradually used for others. The classic example is the social security number in the United States, created in 1936 solely for tracking earnings and paying retirement benefits. Over decades, it became a de facto national identification number, used for credit reporting, tax filing, driver’s licenses, medical records, and countless other purposes. The structure — the SSN system — drifted from a narrow benefits-tracking function to a broad identity-management function, with profound implications for privacy and security.

8.2.3 Constituency Drift

A subtler form of structure drift is constituency drift: when the community or audience that a structure serves changes over time. A scientific journal founded to serve researchers in theoretical physics may, over decades, attract more applied work, engineering papers, and interdisciplinary contributions. The journal’s name and stated scope remain the same, but its actual readership, contributor base, and editorial standards have shifted. This drift is often invisible to insiders but obvious to newcomers who bring expectations based on the journal’s historical reputation.

8.2.4 Problem Drift

Problem drift occurs when the central question or problem that a structure addresses silently changes. This is particularly common in academic disciplines. A field founded to study “intelligence” may, over time, become so focused on measurable cognitive abilities that it ceases to address broader questions of wisdom, creativity, or practical reasoning. The problem has drifted from “what is intelligence?” to “how can we measure variation in cognitive test performance?” — a very different inquiry (Sternberg, 1997).

8.3 Examples of Structure Drift

8.3.1 The Department That Changed Its Purpose

Consider a university department of “Computer Science” founded in the 1960s. Its original purpose was to study the theoretical foundations of computation — algorithms, computability, automata theory. Over the next sixty years, the field expanded dramatically. The same department now teaches web development, data science, human-computer interaction, artificial intelligence, software engineering, and cybersecurity. The name “Computer Science” remains, but the department’s actual identity has drifted from a theoretical discipline to a broad professional school. This is not necessarily wrong — indeed, it may be a healthy response to changing circumstances. But it creates tensions: faculty hired for theoretical work may feel marginalized; students may expect professional training that the department’s culture resists; external stakeholders may hold expectations based on the original identity.

The danger of constituency drift in this context is that the department cannot effectively serve either its original or its evolved mission because it fails to acknowledge the depth of the change. Without explicit recognition of drift, the department tries to be everything to everyone, satisfying no one.

8.3.2 The Framework Designed for Small Systems

In software engineering, one of the most common examples of structure drift is the architectural framework that began its life designed for small-to-medium applications but was gradually applied to enterprise-scale systems. The Ruby on Rails framework, for instance, was conceived in 2004 as a tool for building database-backed web applications quickly and elegantly (Hansson, 2004). It famously emphasized convention over configuration and optimized for developer happiness on modest-scale projects. Over the following decade, Rails was applied to increasingly large and complex systems — GitHub, Shopify, Basecamp itself. The framework adapted, but not without strain. Performance optimizations, concurrency models, and deployment architectures that were irrelevant in Rails’s original context became central concerns. The framework’s identity drifted from a rapid prototyping tool to an enterprise-scale application platform.

The danger of unacknowledged drift in this case is that practitioners make decisions based on a mental model of the framework that is no longer accurate. A startup chooses Rails because “it’s great for rapid prototyping,” but the company ends up spending months dealing with enterprise-scale scaling issues that were never part of Rails’s original design brief, and which other frameworks might have handled more naturally.

8.3.3 The Scientific Taxonomy That Warped Under New Discoveries

Scientific taxonomies are particularly vulnerable to structure drift because they are expected to remain stable while the phenomena they classify proliferate. The periodic table of elements, remarkably, has not drifted: the discovery of new elements has filled preexisting slots rather than requiring structural rearrangement. But most taxonomies are not so fortunate. The classification of living organisms, from Linnaeus’s original system through the cladistic revolution, has undergone repeated structural drift as new species, genetic relationships, and evolutionary theories have reshaped the categories (Ereshefsky, 2001).

More troubling is the case of diagnostic taxonomies in psychology and psychiatry. The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) has undergone profound structural drift across its five major editions. The DSM-I (1952) was heavily influenced by psychoanalytic theory; the DSM-III (1980) represented a radical shift to a descriptive, atheoretical approach; the DSM-5 (2013) attempted to incorporate dimensional and neurobiological perspectives. Each edition is still called “the DSM” — the structure claims continuity — but the actual architecture of classification has changed fundamentally (Horwitz & Wakefield, 2007). Practitioners who rely on the DSM must navigate a structure whose identity has drifted significantly, often without explicit acknowledgment of what was lost or gained in each transition.

8.4 The Danger of Unnoticed Scope Creep

The common thread running through these examples is the danger of unnoticed scope creep. When drift is recognized and discussed, it can be managed. When it goes unnoticed, it creates a series of pathologies.

8.4.1 Identity Confusion

A structure that has drifted without acknowledgment suffers from identity confusion. It does not know what it is anymore. The organization is pulled in multiple directions; the framework is applied to problems it was never designed for; the taxonomy is stretched to cover phenomena it cannot coherently contain. This confusion is experienced by practitioners as a vague sense that “something isn’t right” — a feeling that the structure is no longer fit for purpose, even though no one can articulate exactly what has gone wrong.

8.4.2 Decision Paralysis

Unacknowledged drift makes decision-making difficult. Should the department hire a theoretical computer scientist or a data engineer? Should the Rails application adopt a microservices architecture or invest in better caching? These questions are unanswerable without a clear understanding of what the structure is actually for. When identity is muddled, every decision becomes contested, and every strategy appears equally valid — or equally invalid.

8.4.3 Ineffective Communication

A drifted structure communicates poorly with its environment. Stakeholders hold expectations based on the structure’s stated identity, but the structure delivers something different. Customers of an organization may expect one thing based on its mission statement; employees deliver something else based on the organization’s actual evolved practice. This gap erodes trust and generates friction.

8.5 Auditing a Structure for Drift

How can we detect structure drift before it becomes pathological? The answer is regular, systematic auditing. A structural audit for drift asks a different set of questions than an audit for failure signals (Chapter 4). It is concerned not with how well the structure performs its functions but with whether those functions are the ones the structure was meant to perform.

8.5.1 The Origin Audit

The first step in detecting drift is understanding where the structure began. This requires:

  1. Recovering the original purpose. What problem was this structure created to solve? What question was it designed to answer? What function was it intended to serve? Foundational documents, mission statements, design rationales, and interviews with original participants can help reconstruct this original intent.

  2. Identifying the original scope. What was the structure explicitly designed NOT to do? What boundaries were intentionally set? Understanding a structure’s original limitations is as important as understanding its aspirations.

  3. Reconstructing the original context. What were the conditions — technical, social, organizational — that shaped the structure’s creation? Structures designed in one era may become misaligned not because they have changed but because the world has changed around them.

8.5.2 The Current-State Audit

The second step is mapping the structure’s current identity:

  1. Observing actual practice. What does the structure actually do? Not what does its documentation say it does, but what do people use it for? Where do they apply it? How do they talk about it?

  2. Mapping resource allocation. Where does the structure invest its energy? In organizations, this means following the budget: which functions receive the most funding and staffing? In software, this means following the development roadmap and issue tracker.

  3. Surveying stakeholder expectations. What do different groups — users, beneficiaries, participants — expect from the structure? Are these expectations aligned with each other? Are they aligned with the structure’s stated purpose?

8.5.3 The Gap Analysis

The third step is comparing origin with current state. This gap analysis asks:

  • Has the structure’s primary function changed? Is it now doing something fundamentally different from what it was created to do?
  • Has the structure’s scope expanded or contracted? Does it now cover more (or less) territory than originally intended?
  • Has the structure’s community changed? Does it serve a different constituency?
  • Has the structure’s core question or problem shifted? Is it now addressing a different inquiry?

The answers to these questions reveal whether drift has occurred, how extensive it is, and whether it has been beneficial or harmful.

8.6 Regular Health Checks: A Living Architecture Practice

Detecting drift should not be a one-time exercise. It should be a regular practice — part of the maintenance of any living architecture. We recommend structural health checks conducted at regular intervals (annually for most organizations and frameworks) or triggered by significant events (major new use cases, community growth, leadership changes).

8.6.1 What to Look For

A structural health check should examine:

  1. Mission alignment. Are the structure’s activities aligned with its stated mission? Or has there been silent mission creep?

  2. Boundary clarity. Can practitioners clearly articulate what is inside and outside the structure’s scope? Are boundary disputes common?

  3. Identity consistency. Does the structure present the same identity to different audiences, or does it shape-shift depending on context?

  4. Evolution trajectory. Is the structure drifting in a direction that its stakeholders endorse, or is it being pulled by external forces that no one has consciously chosen?

8.6.2 Tools and Techniques

Health checks can use a variety of tools:

  • Stakeholder interviews to surface divergent expectations
  • Documentation reviews to track how the structure’s self-description has changed over time
  • Usage analytics to see how the structure is actually employed
  • Scenario testing — presenting hypothetical new cases and asking whether the structure would handle them coherently
  • Historical trace — reconstructing the sequence of decisions that led to the current state

8.7 Re-centering versus Accommodating Drift

When drift is detected, the structure’s custodians face a choice: re-center the structure on its original purpose, or accommodate the drift as a legitimate evolution. Neither answer is inherently correct; both have costs and benefits.

8.7.1 Re-centering

Re-centering involves deliberately returning the structure to its original purpose, scope, and identity. This may mean discontinuing functions that have drifted away from the core, refocusing resources, and recommunicating the structure’s mission.

When to re-center: When drift has been caused by neglect, resource opportunism, or external pressures that do not reflect genuine need. When the original purpose remains valid and valuable. When the drifted functions are better served by other structures.

Risks of re-centering: Re-centering can appear rigid and backward-looking. It may alienate stakeholders who have come to rely on the drifted functions. It may ignore genuine evolution in the structure’s environment that makes the original purpose less relevant.

8.7.2 Accommodating Drift

Accommodating drift means explicitly acknowledging that the structure has changed, updating its formal description to match its actual operation, and embracing the new identity.

When to accommodate: When the drift reflects genuine adaptation to changing circumstances. When the new functions are more valuable than the original ones. When the original purpose has become obsolete or less important.

Risks of accommodating: Accommodating can legitimize mission creep that dilutes the structure’s effectiveness. It may allow the structure to become a “jack of all trades, master of none.” It may disappoint stakeholders who valued the original identity.

8.7.3 A Pragmatic Approach

The most effective approach combines elements of both responses. Structures should honor their origin while staying responsive to their environment. This means:

  • Explicitly acknowledging drift rather than pretending it hasn’t happened
  • Separating core from periphery — identifying which functions are essential to the structure’s identity and which are accretions that can be shed
  • Choosing deliberately — making conscious decisions about identity rather than allowing drift to continue unnoticed
  • Communicating clearly — ensuring that all stakeholders understand what the structure is and is not

A living architecture is not one that never drifts; it is one that drifts with awareness and intention.

8.8 Key Takeaways

  • Structure drift is the silent shift in a framework’s object, purpose, scope, or identity over time. It happens through scope creep, function creep, constituency drift, and problem drift.
  • The danger of unnoticed drift includes identity confusion, decision paralysis, and ineffective communication with stakeholders.
  • Detecting drift requires a three-part audit: understanding the structure’s origin, mapping its current state, and analyzing the gap between them.
  • Regular health checks — systematic reviews of mission alignment, boundary clarity, identity consistency, and evolution trajectory — should be a routine practice for any living architecture.
  • When drift is detected, the choice between re-centering and accommodating drift depends on whether the drift reflects genuine adaptation or mere accretion. The most effective response combines explicit acknowledgment, deliberate choice, and clear communication.

8.9 Exercises and Reflections

  1. Select a structure you are intimately familiar with — a team, a department, a software framework, or a theoretical model. Conduct a drift audit: recover its original purpose, map its current state, and analyze the gap. Has the structure drifted? Is the drift productive or problematic?

  2. Identify a case where a structure’s drift went unnoticed and caused problems. What were the early signals that drift was occurring? Why were they missed? What would a regular health check have revealed?

  3. Design a structural health check for your organization or field. What indicators would you monitor? How frequently would you conduct the check? Who would be involved? What would trigger a re-centering decision versus an accommodation decision? Draft a one-page protocol for this health check.

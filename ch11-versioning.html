13  Chapter 11 — Versioning

“Every great work of art has two faces: one toward its own time and one toward the future.”
— Martha Graham

“Things change. That’s just the way it is. But when you have a system based on consensus and shared understanding, you need a way to track those changes without losing your mind.”
— Attributed to Tim Berners-Lee

13.1 The Need for a Map of Structural Time

If iteration is the engine of structural evolution, then versioning is the cartography. It provides the maps, milestones, and markers that allow us to navigate the landscape of a changing structure. Without versioning, iteration produces confusion: users of a framework cannot tell which version they are working with, developers cannot trace when a particular change was introduced, and the community cannot coordinate around a shared reference point.

Versioning is, at its core, a response to a fundamental tension in the lifecycle of any living structure. On one hand, the structure must evolve to remain relevant—to incorporate new knowledge, fix flaws, and adapt to changing conditions. On the other hand, the structure must provide stability so that its users can rely on it. A software library that changes every week without warning is unusable; a scientific taxonomy that is revised annually without documentation is untrustworthy; a business process that is restructured monthly without version tracking creates chaos.

Versioning resolves this tension by naming time. It assigns identifiers to specific states of a structure at specific points in its evolution, creating a shared vocabulary that allows us to refer to “how things were” and “how things are” with precision.

This chapter explores why versioning matters for conceptual structures, how different versioning schemes work and when to use them, and the practices—change logs, migration documentation, semantic conventions—that make versioning a tool for clarity rather than bureaucracy.

13.2 Why Versioning Matters for Conceptual Structures

Versioning is so deeply embedded in software development that its broader applicability is often overlooked. Yet every living conceptual structure—every framework, model, taxonomy, or theory that undergoes iterative change—faces versioning challenges.

13.2.1 When Theories Need Versions

Consider a scientific theory that evolves over time. The theory of evolution by natural selection has undergone profound changes since Darwin’s On the Origin of Species in 1859: the incorporation of Mendelian genetics (the modern synthesis), the discovery of DNA as the mechanism of inheritance, the emergence of evolutionary developmental biology (evo-devo). Scientists speak of “Darwin’s theory,” “the modern synthesis,” and “the extended evolutionary synthesis” as distinct versions of the same underlying theory structure. These version labels enable precise discourse: a biologist who says “I am working within the framework of the modern synthesis” is communicating something specific about which assumptions she holds and which mechanisms she considers central.

Without versioning, the theory would exist as an undifferentiated blob of propositions, with no way to refer to its historical states. Students would be taught a confused amalgam of 1859 and 2024 ideas. The development of the theory—the very thing we care about—would be invisible.

13.2.2 The Coordination Function

Versioning serves a coordination function that is especially critical when a structure is used by multiple people or groups. If a team of architects is working from “version 2.1” of a reference architecture, they can share assumptions, build consistent artifacts, and communicate without ambiguity. If two economists are debating “the Phillips curve,” but one is referring to the original 1958 formulation and the other to the 1970s expectations-augmented version, their conversation will be riddled with misunderstanding.

In this sense, versioning is a social technology—a tool for aligning the mental models of distributed collaborators across time.

13.2.3 The Accountability Function

Versioning also serves accountability. When a structure changes, it is important to know what was changed, by whom, and why. This is not merely a bureaucratic concern. In safety-critical domains—medical guidelines, aviation procedures, financial regulations—the ability to trace which version of a protocol was in effect at a given time can be a matter of life and death. And in intellectual domains, versioning provides the foundation for attributing credit: who introduced a particular concept, and in which iteration of the framework did that concept appear?

13.3 Versioning Schemes

Different contexts call for different versioning approaches. The choice of scheme reflects the nature of the structure, the rate of change, and the needs of its users.

13.3.1 Semantic Versioning

Borrowed from software distribution, semantic versioning (often abbreviated SemVer) uses a three-part number: MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH. The rules are:

  • MAJOR version increments when incompatible changes are made (breaking changes).
  • MINOR version increments when functionality is added in a backward-compatible manner.
  • PATCH version increments when backward-compatible bug fixes or corrections are made.

SemVer was formalized by Tom Preston-Werner in 2013 and has become the dominant versioning scheme in open-source software ecosystems. Its genius lies in encoding the nature of the change in the version number itself. A user seeing that a library has gone from 2.3.1 to 2.4.0 knows they can upgrade without breaking their code; if it goes to 3.0.0, they know to prepare for disruption.

Applicability beyond software. The SemVer philosophy can be adapted to other domains. A theoretical framework could adopt similar conventions:

  • Major version: A change that invalidates previous applications of the framework. New concepts are introduced that redefine or supersede old ones. Prior work based on the old version may need reinterpretation.
  • Minor version: New elements, categories, or propositions are added, but the core structure remains intact. Prior work remains valid, though possibly incomplete.
  • Patch version: Corrections to errors in articulation, clarification of ambiguous definitions, or addition of examples. No structural change.

The International Classification of Diseases (ICD), maintained by the World Health Organization, uses a form of semantic versioning: ICD-10 and ICD-11 represent major revisions with significant structural changes, while updates within a version are designated by more granular codes.

Limitations. SemVer assumes a clear definition of “backward compatibility,” which is straightforward in software (does the old API still work?) but more ambiguous in conceptual structures (does the old interpretation still hold?). Defining what constitutes a “breaking change” in a theoretical model requires careful judgment.

13.3.2 Chronological Versioning

Chronological versioning assigns versions based on dates or sequential numbers in order of release. Examples include:

  • Date-based: 2024-Q1, 2024-Q2 (quarterly releases)
  • Sequential: v1, v2, v3, v4 (simple sequential numbers)
  • Hybrid: 2024.1, 2024.2 (year + sequence)

Chronological versioning is simpler than semantic versioning. It communicates when a version was released, not what kind of change it contains. This is appropriate for structures where the nature of change is less important than the timeline of evolution—for example, annual updates to a national curriculum framework or quarterly releases of a design system.

The trade-off is obvious: a user of a chronologically versioned framework knows how recent the version is, but not whether upgrading will be painful. Two consecutive versions might differ trivially or fundamentally. The user must consult the change log to determine the significance of the change.

13.3.3 Milestone-Based Versioning

Some structures are best versioned by milestone—a label that marks a significant achievement or stage in development. Common examples include:

  • Alpha/Beta/Release Candidate/Release — Indicates maturity and stability level
  • Draft/Proposal/Ratified/Revised — Common in standards bodies and policy frameworks
  • Edition — Used for textbooks and reference works (e.g., “Third Edition”)

Milestone-based labels are less precise than numbers, but they convey important information about the status of a version. A “draft” framework is understood to be preliminary and subject to major change. A “ratified” standard carries the weight of consensus and should be treated as authoritative.

In academic contexts, the distinction between a “working paper” and a “published article” is a form of milestone-based versioning, signaling the degree of vetting the work has undergone.

13.3.4 Choosing a Scheme

The choice of versioning scheme depends on the answers to three questions:

  1. Who uses this structure? Users who need stable, predictable evolution (e.g., software developers, regulated industries) benefit from semantic versioning. Users who care primarily about currency (e.g., policy analysts, trend watchers) may prefer chronological versioning.

  2. How fast does the structure change? Rapidly evolving structures need a versioning scheme that can keep pace. Slowly evolving structures can manage with milestone labels.

  3. What information matters most? If the nature of change is critical, use semantic versioning. If the timing of change is critical, use chronological versioning. If the maturity of the structure is critical, use milestone-based versioning.

In practice, many structures use a hybrid approach. A framework might have major versions numbered 1, 2, 3 (semantic), release dates for minor updates (chronological), and alpha/beta labels for development snapshots (milestone).

13.4 What Constitutes a Major vs. Minor Version Change

One of the most practically significant—and most debated—aspects of versioning is determining what qualifies as a “major” change. This determination requires judgment, and different communities will draw the line differently.

13.4.1 In Software Architecture

In software, a major version change typically involves:

  • Removal of an existing API or feature
  • Change in the fundamental data model or storage format
  • Change in the minimum system requirements (e.g., dropping support for an operating system)
  • Redesign of the user interface that invalidates training materials

A minor version change typically involves:

  • Addition of new API endpoints or features
  • Deprecation (but not removal) of old features
  • Performance improvements that do not change behavior
  • Expanded documentation

A patch involves:

  • Bug fixes
  • Security patches
  • Minor performance tweaks

13.4.2 In Conceptual Frameworks

Extending these ideas to non-software structures, we can propose analogous guidelines:

Major version change (e.g., Framework v1 → v2): - Introduction of a new foundational concept that reshapes the entire framework - Removal or fundamental redefinition of a core concept - Change in the relationship structure between concepts (e.g., from hierarchical to network-based) - Rejection of a previously accepted proposition or principle

Minor version change (e.g., Framework v1.0 → v1.1): - Addition of a new concept that extends but does not disrupt the existing structure - Clarification of ambiguous definitions - New case studies or examples - Expansion into a new domain of application

Patch (e.g., Framework v1.0.0 → v1.0.1): - Correction of factual errors in examples - Fixing of typographical or formatting errors - Updating references to reflect current sources - Minor editorial clarifications

13.4.3 The Problem of “Minor” That Is Actually Major

A persistent challenge is that changes declared as “minor” sometimes have major implications for users. A framework that adds a new concept may, in practice, shift the understanding of existing concepts. A software library that simply “adds a new feature” may implicitly change recommended usage patterns.

The best safeguard against this misalignment is transparency in the change log (discussed below). Even if a change is labeled minor, the accompanying documentation should describe its implications honestly, allowing users to make their own assessment.

13.5 Change Logs and Migration Documentation

A version number is only as useful as the documentation that accompanies it. A version number without a change log is a headline without an article.

13.5.1 The Change Log

A change log is a curated, chronologically ordered list of changes for each version of a project. The excellent Keep a Changelog convention (Olivier Lacan, 2014) proposes a standard format with sections for:

  • Added — New features or elements
  • Changed — Modifications to existing elements
  • Deprecated — Elements that will be removed in a future version
  • Removed — Elements removed in this version
  • Fixed — Bug or error corrections
  • Security — Security-related changes

For conceptual structures, these categories translate directly:

  • Added: New concepts, principles, categories, or relationships
  • Changed: Revised definitions, modified relationships, updated examples
  • Deprecated: Concepts or terms that are being phased out
  • Removed: Concepts or terms that have been eliminated
  • Fixed: Corrections to errors in earlier versions

A good change log answers the question: “What do I need to know to decide whether to upgrade?” It should be written for humans, not machines. It should link to more detailed documentation where appropriate.

13.5.2 Migration Documentation

For major version changes, a change log is insufficient. Users need migration documentation—a guided walkthrough of the differences between versions and instructions for transitioning.

Migration documentation for a conceptual framework should include:

  1. Summary of changes. A high-level overview of what has changed and why.
  2. Concept mapping. A table showing how concepts in the old version correspond (or do not) to concepts in the new version.
  3. Impact analysis. What changes when you move from the old version to the new? Applications, use cases, and interpretations that were valid under the old version—are they still valid?
  4. Transition examples. Worked examples showing how to translate work done under the old version into the terms of the new version.
  5. Deprecation timeline. If elements have been deprecated but not yet removed, when will they be removed? What should users do in the meantime?

13.5.3 The Changelog as Intellectual History

Beyond its pragmatic function, a changelog serves as a record of intellectual development. For a living structure, the changelog is its autobiography—the story of how it grew, what problems it encountered, what decisions were made, and why.

This historical dimension is often undervalued. But understanding why a structure evolved as it did can be as valuable as understanding the structure itself. A user who reads the change log of a framework from version 1 to version 10 gains insight into the framework’s priorities, its blind spots, and the evolving needs of its community. This understanding makes the user a better interpreter and critic of the framework.

13.6 Examples Across Domains

13.6.1 Software Versioning

The most mature versioning culture exists in software. The Linux kernel, for instance, has used a variety of versioning schemes over its history. Early versions used a simple numbering (0.01, 0.02, …), later a major.minor.patchlist structure with even-numbered minor versions indicating stable releases and odd numbers indicating development releases. This scheme encoded stability information directly in the version number—a form of communication that went beyond simple identification.

The Python programming language’s transition from Python 2 to Python 3 (2008) is a landmark case in versioning and migration. The change was explicitly major: the removal of long-standing features, changes to fundamental string handling, and incompatibility with most existing Python 2 code. The version number 3.0 communicated unequivocally that this was a break. The long period of coexistence (Python 2 was not officially retired until 2020) accompanied by detailed migration documentation and tooling, demonstrates the importance of supporting users through major transitions.

13.6.2 Specification Management

The OpenAPI Specification (formerly known as the Swagger Specification) offers a model of disciplined versioning for a conceptual structure. The specification defines a standard for describing REST APIs, and its evolution has been managed through a formal versioning process.

OpenAPI 2.0 (2014) was a major revision of the earlier Swagger 2.0. OpenAPI 3.0 (2017) introduced significant structural changes, including a new component system and improved support for hypermedia. The migration from 2.0 to 3.0 was documented in detail, and the specification maintained an official migration guide. What is notable is that the OpenAPI community treated the specification itself—a conceptual structure—as a versioned artifact, with all the discipline usually reserved for software releases.

13.6.3 Theoretical Development

In academic theory, versioning is less formalized but no less present. Consider the evolution of Maslow’s hierarchy of needs. The original 1943 paper proposed five levels (physiological, safety, love, esteem, self-actualization). In subsequent decades, Maslow himself and later researchers proposed revisions: adding transcendence as a level beyond self-actualization (Maslow, 1969), questioning the strict hierarchical ordering, and distinguishing between deficiency needs and growth needs.

Scholars who discuss “Maslow’s hierarchy” today are often unaware which version they are referencing. The lack of explicit versioning leads to confusion: critiques of the original 1943 model may not apply to later formulations, and practitioners applying the model may be working from an outdated version without knowing it. This is a cautionary tale about the costs of implicit versioning.

13.7 The Balance Between Stability and Evolution

Versioning is ultimately a tool for managing the fundamental tension between stability and evolution. A structure that never changes provides perfect stability but eventually becomes irrelevant. A structure that changes constantly provides perfect responsiveness but never stabilizes enough to be usable.

Versioning does not resolve this tension. It manages it. By providing clear markers of what has changed and when, versioning allows different users to choose their own relationship to structural change:

  • Risk-averse users can stay on stable, older versions and migrate only when necessary.
  • Early adopters can move to new versions quickly, accepting instability in exchange for access to improvements.
  • Maintainers can make breaking changes when needed, knowing that users will be warned through version numbers and migration documentation.

The art of good versioning lies in understanding which changes are genuinely necessary, communicating them clearly, and supporting users through transitions.

13.8 Key Takeaways

  • Versioning is the practice of assigning identifiers to specific states of a structure, enabling precise reference across time and among users.
  • Versioning serves coordination (aligning collaborators), accountability (tracing changes), and communication (conveying the nature of change).
  • Semantic versioning (MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH) encodes the nature of change in the version number and is well-suited to structures where backward compatibility matters.
  • Chronological versioning communicates timing and is simpler but less informative about change type.
  • Milestone-based versioning (alpha, beta, draft, ratified) communicates maturity and status.
  • Determining what constitutes a major vs. minor change requires domain-specific judgment and transparent documentation.
  • Change logs and migration documentation are essential companions to version numbers, providing the detail needed for informed decisions.
  • The versioning scheme should reflect the structure’s domain, rate of change, and user community’s needs.
  • Implicit versioning (changes without labels) causes confusion and undermines the utility of a living structure.

13.9 Exercises and Reflections

  1. Version a theory or framework in your field. Pick a well-known theory or model in your domain. Research its evolution and construct a version history with major, minor, and patch designations. What events marked major versions? Was there a breaking change that rendered earlier formulations incomplete or incorrect?

  2. Design a versioning policy for a living document. Imagine you are responsible for a reference architecture or conceptual framework that will evolve over time. Draft a versioning policy that specifies: (a) what constitutes a major/minor/patch change, (b) how versions will be numbered, (c) how and when migration documentation will be provided, and (d) how long old versions will be supported.

  3. Audit the change log culture in your organization or discipline. Do the frameworks and models you use have change logs? Are they accessible and readable? What information do they convey? Propose an improvement to the versioning or documentation practice of a structure you work with.
